## Executive Summary

Mariano Fernandez (born 1905) died on **January 10, 1945** in **Gerona, Tarlac** — exactly one day after the Allied landings at Lingayen Gulf launched the Battle of Luzon. This research reconstructs the military situation in Gerona on that date. The findings paint a picture of a town caught in the violent collision between retreating Japanese forces and advancing American/Filipino troops, with civilians — like Mariano — trapped in the crossfire.

**Key conclusions:**
1. Gerona was directly on the main highway (now MacArthur Highway) between Lingayen Gulf and Manila — the primary axis of both retreat and advance
2. The 21st Division (USAFFE) was **garrisoned at and around Gerona** in late December 1941, making it a militarily significant town throughout the war
3. Col. Edwin Ramsey's **East Central Luzon Guerrilla Area (ECLGA)** — ~40,000 fighters — operated from Manila to Lingayen Gulf, encompassing Gerona
4. On January 9, 1945, XIV Corps and I Corps landed at Lingayen Gulf, ~60 km north of Gerona. Japanese forces began retreating southward through the Central Luzon plain — directly through Gerona
5. January 10 was **Day 2** of the invasion — Japanese forces were in panicked retreat and known to commit atrocities against civilians during withdrawals
6. Mariano's death aligns with the documented pattern of Japanese reprisal killings against civilians suspected of aiding guerrillas

## 1. The Battle of Luzon — What Happened on January 9-10, 1945

### S-Day: January 9, 1945
- **0700:** Pre-assault naval bombardment from ~70 battleships and cruisers at Lingayen Gulf
- **0800:** U.S. 6th Army (Gen. Walter Krueger) began landing at Lingayen Gulf
- **~68,000 troops** landed on Day 1 along a 20-mile (32 km) beachhead between Lingayen and San Fabian
- Total invasion force: **203,608 soldiers**, ~800 ships
- Two assault forces:
  - **XIV Corps** (MGen Oscar Griswold) — Western landing area at Lingayen town
    - 37th Infantry Division ("Buckeye")
    - 40th Infantry Division ("Sunshine")
  - **I Corps** (MGen Innis P. Swift) — Eastern landing area at San Fabian
    - 43rd Infantry Division ("Winged Victory")
    - 6th Infantry Division ("Red Star")

### January 10, 1945 — The Day Mariano Died
- U.S. forces were rapidly expanding the beachhead, penetrating up to **5 miles inland** from the coast
- XIV Corps began its advance **south toward Manila** along the Central Luzon plain
- The main route south followed **Highway 3** (now MacArthur Highway) — which runs directly through **Gerona, Tarlac**
- Japanese forces of Gen. Yamashita's **Shobu Group** (~152,000 troops in northern Luzon) began withdrawing to mountain positions
- The **Kembu Group** (~30,000 troops in central Luzon) was positioned around Clark Air Base, south of Gerona
- **Gerona sat between the two Japanese force groups** — on the main highway connecting them

### The Geography: Why Gerona Was Ground Zero
Gerona is located:
- **~60 km south** of Lingayen Gulf (the landing beaches)
- **~14 km north** of Tarlac City (the provincial capital)
- **On the MacArthur Highway** — the primary north-south route through Central Luzon
- Bounded by Paniqui (north), Pura (east), Tarlac City (south), Santa Ignacia (west)

This placement made Gerona a **critical chokepoint** for Japanese forces retreating from the Lingayen area toward their defensive positions at Clark Air Base and in the mountains.

## 2. The 21st Division USAFFE — Gerona's Wartime History (1941-1942)

### The Division Was Garrisoned Next to Gerona
The 21st Infantry Division (Philippine Commonwealth Army) was one of the ten reserve divisions mobilized under USAFFE. Critically:

- **Mobilized:** November 18, 1941
- **Commander:** BGen Mateo M. Capinpin
- **Garrison:** Camp Malatiqui, **Santa Ignacia, Tarlac** — the town immediately west of Gerona
- **Mobilization area:** Provinces of **Tarlac, Pangasinan, La Union, and Nueva Ecija**
- **Strength:** ~8,500 men

### The Retreat Through Gerona (December 1941)
After the Japanese landed at Lingayen Gulf on December 22, 1941, Gen. Wainwright established five defensive lines across the Central Luzon plain:

| Date | Defensive Line | 21st Division Position |
|------|---------------|----------------------|
| Dec 24 | D-1 | Aguilar to **San Carlos, Pangasinan** |
| Dec 26 | D-2 | **Camiling, Tarlac** (left flank) |
| Dec 27 | D-3 | **Sta. Ignacia and Gerona, Tarlac** |
| Dec 30 | D-4 | Road to Bamban |
| Dec 31 | D-5 | Fort Stotsenburg |

**The 21st Division was specifically stationed at Gerona on D-3 (December 27, 1941).**

This means:
- Gerona residents witnessed the full retreat of Filipino-American forces through their town
- The Fernandez family in Barangay Danzo would have seen soldiers passing through
- Many soldiers from the 21st Division were recruited from Tarlac and Pangasinan — the same provinces as the Fernandez family
- Men of fighting age (like Mariano, age 36 in 1941) may have been recruited or volunteered

### San Carlos Connection
The 21st Division's D-1 defensive line included **San Carlos, Pangasinan** — the Fernandez family's town of origin. The division's recruitment area was Tarlac-Pangasinan, meaning the Fernandez family network was directly in the mobilization zone.

## 3. Guerrilla Operations: The ECLGA and Ramsey's Network

### Edwin Ramsey and the East Central Luzon Guerrilla Area
After the fall of Bataan (April 1942), Lt. Edwin Ramsey escaped to central Luzon and joined the guerrilla resistance:

- **Claude Thorp** was initially given the task of organizing guerrilla resistance by MacArthur
- Luzon was divided into four guerrilla regions
- **Joseph Barker** was given responsibility for the **East Central Luzon Guerrilla Area (ECLGA)**, extending from **Manila to Lingayen Gulf**
- After Thorp was captured (October 1942), Barker took his place
- After Barker was caught and executed by the Japanese, **Ramsey took command of the ECLGA**
- The ECLGA grew to nearly **40,000 fighters**
- They fought with captured and improvised weapons, gathered intelligence, and distributed propaganda

### Gerona's Place in the ECLGA
The ECLGA's territory — Manila to Lingayen Gulf — **directly encompassed Gerona, Tarlac**. Gerona is roughly in the center of this territory. The confirmed facts about the Fernandez family's connections strongly suggest guerrilla involvement:

- **Jack Laureano Soriano** from San Carlos was CO of the 1st Battalion under Ramsey's guerrillas
- San Carlos is the Fernandez family's ancestral home
- The Fernandez family had **two tenientes del barrio** (Eulalia and Exequiel Fernandez) — indicating principalía status
- Principalía families across the Philippines frequently led or supported guerrilla resistance
- Ramsey's guerrillas specifically operated in the San Carlos/Gerona area

### The Hukbalahap Connection
The **Hukbalahap** (People's Army Against the Japanese) was another major guerrilla force in Central Luzon:
- Headquartered in **Pampanga** (south of Tarlac)
- Active throughout **Central Luzon** including Tarlac province
- Founded March 1942 in Cabiao, Nueva Ecija (east of Tarlac)
- Communist-led but broad-based peasant resistance movement
- Had significant presence in the agricultural plains of Tarlac

Both the ECLGA and Hukbalahap operated in the Gerona area, though they often had tensions between them.

## 4. How Mariano Likely Died: Reconstructing January 10, 1945

### The Scenario
On January 9-10, 1945, Gerona was in an extremely dangerous situation:

1. **American forces landed 60 km to the north** at Lingayen Gulf
2. **Japanese forces began retreating southward** through the Central Luzon plain
3. **Retreating Japanese troops were known to commit atrocities** against Filipino civilians, especially:
   - Suspected guerrilla supporters
   - Families of local leaders (tenientes del barrio)
   - Residents along the main highway
4. **Guerrilla forces were activated** to support the American advance, conducting sabotage and intelligence operations
5. **Artillery and air strikes** targeted Japanese positions and transportation routes

### Possible Causes of Death

**Most Likely: Japanese Reprisal Killing**
- Japanese forces retreating through Central Luzon committed widespread atrocities against civilians
- The Manila massacre (starting February 3, 1945) killed 100,000-500,000 — but atrocities began **before** Manila, in the provinces
- Japanese orders explicitly stated: "All people on the battlefield with the exception of Japanese military personnel... will be put to death"
- As a member of a principalía family with tenientes del barrio, Mariano would have been a prime target for Japanese reprisal
- Japanese forces specifically targeted suspected guerrilla supporters

**Possible: Combat Crossfire**
- XIV Corps was advancing south from Lingayen along the highway through Gerona
- Japanese forces were retreating and setting up defensive positions
- Civilians caught between advancing and retreating forces suffered heavily
- Artillery bombardments and air strikes hit areas along the main highway

**Possible: Guerrilla-Related Combat**
- If Mariano was actively involved with the ECLGA or local guerrilla units, he may have been killed in combat with Japanese forces
- Guerrilla forces were activated to support the American landings on S-Day
- Local guerrillas would have been conducting operations to disrupt Japanese retreat

### The Pattern of January 1945 Atrocities
Throughout the Philippines, the Japanese retreat in January-March 1945 was accompanied by:
- Wholesale burning of towns and villages
- Mass executions of civilians
- Special targeting of local leaders and their families
- Destruction of food stores and livestock
- Use of Filipino civilians as human shields
- The killing of approximately **150,000 Filipino civilians** during the entire Battle of Luzon, "overwhelmingly civilians who were murdered by Japanese forces"

## 5. The Japanese Occupation of Gerona (1942-1945)

### Three Years Under Occupation
From January 1942 to January 1945, Gerona was under Japanese military control:

- The Japanese established a military administration throughout the Philippines
- The only permitted political party was the **KALIBAPI** (Japanese-organized)
- **"Comfort stations"** were established at Japanese military installations across the Philippines
- Filipino civilians were forced into labor details
- The Japanese-sponsored **Second Philippine Republic** under Jose P. Laurel nominally governed, but military authority prevailed
- Japanese "Mickey Mouse money" (occupation currency) replaced real pesos, causing economic hardship

### Resistance in Gerona
Despite the occupation:
- The ECLGA maintained an underground network through the area
- Local leaders (like tenientes del barrio) often served dual roles — cooperating with Japanese authorities while secretly aiding guerrillas
- Intelligence networks fed information to MacArthur's command via submarine radio contact
- By 1944, guerrilla forces controlled approximately **60% of the Philippine islands** (mostly forested and mountainous areas)

### Camp O'Donnell Connection
After the Bataan Death March (April 1942), approximately 80,000 prisoners of war were forced to march to **Camp O'Donnell in Capas, Tarlac** — only about 30 km south of Gerona. Thousands died on the march and in the camp. Gerona residents would have witnessed the death march passing through or near their area.

## 6. Barangay Danzo During WWII

### What We Know
- Danzo is one of 44 barangays of Gerona, located in the eastern (plain, low-lying agricultural) area
- Founded by migrants from **Pangasinan** (confirmed by the Fernandez family's San Carlos origin)
- Had its own elementary school (Danzo Elementary School — listed on Wikipedia)
- The Fernandez family held positions as **tenientes del barrio** — the highest local government authority at the barangay level

### During the War
- As a small agricultural barangay on the plain, Danzo would have been relatively exposed to military movements
- The eastern part of Gerona (where Danzo is located) consists of "plain, low-lying agricultural land" — offering little natural cover
- Japanese forces would have controlled movement through the barangays
- Food requisitioning and forced labor would have affected farming communities like Danzo
- The Tarlac River cuts through Gerona, dividing it into two parts — Danzo's position relative to this river would have been strategically relevant during the retreat

## 7. US Military Units That Passed Through Gerona

### January 1945 Liberation
The following US and Filipino units were involved in the advance through Central Luzon:

**XIV Corps** (advancing south from Lingayen toward Manila):
- **37th Infantry Division** ("Buckeye") — Ohio National Guard
- **40th Infantry Division** ("Sunshine") — California/Nevada/Utah National Guard
- Both divisions landed at the western beaches near Lingayen town
- Advanced south along Highway 3 (MacArthur Highway) — through Gerona

**I Corps** (protecting the eastern flank):
- **43rd Infantry Division** ("Winged Victory")
- **6th Infantry Division** ("Red Star")
- Landed at San Fabian, advanced east and south

### The Race to Clark Air Base
After the landings, XIV Corps advanced rapidly south. The first significant Japanese resistance was encountered at **Clark Air Base** on January 23 — about 60 km south of Gerona. This means Gerona was likely liberated (or at least passed through) sometime between **January 10-15, 1945**.

### Filipino Guerrilla Units
- **ECLGA** (Ramsey's command) — up to 40,000 fighters, Manila to Lingayen
- **Hukbalahap** — active throughout Central Luzon
- **USAFIP (NL)** — United States Army Forces in the Philippines, Northern Luzon (operated further north but had contacts in Tarlac)

## 10. The Fernandez Family's Wartime Experience — Narrative Reconstruction

### December 1941: The War Comes to Gerona
When the Japanese landed at Lingayen Gulf on December 22, 1941, the 21st Division — recruited from Tarlac and Pangasinan — was stationed at Gerona as part of Defensive Line D-3. Mariano Fernandez, age 36, with his wife Victoriana Daquigan and 3-year-old son Bienvenido, would have watched Filipino-American soldiers retreat through their town. Many of these soldiers would have been men they knew — neighbors, relatives, fellow Pangasinenses.

### 1942-1944: Occupation
For three years, the Fernandez family lived under Japanese occupation. As a teniente del barrio family, they would have navigated the dangerous dual role of appearing to cooperate with Japanese authorities while potentially supporting the growing guerrilla movement. The ECLGA under Ramsey was building a 40,000-strong network across Central Luzon. Through the San Carlos connection (Jack Laureano Soriano commanding the 1st Battalion), the Fernandez family had direct links to the guerrilla command structure.

### January 9, 1945: Liberation Begins
When the American bombardment of Lingayen Gulf began, the sound of naval guns would have been audible in Gerona, 60 km to the south. For the guerrillas, this was the signal they had waited three years for — the Americans were coming back. Japanese forces began their panicked retreat southward along Highway 3, passing directly through Gerona.

### January 10, 1945: Mariano's Death
On the second day of the invasion, as Japanese forces streamed through Gerona in retreat, Mariano Fernandez was killed. He was 40 years old. His son Bienvenido was 7. The exact circumstances — whether Japanese reprisal, combat crossfire, or guerrilla action — remain unknown, but the historical context makes Japanese violence against civilians the most likely cause.

Victoriana Daquigan was left a widow with a young son in a war-torn town. The liberation of Gerona by XIV Corps likely came within days of Mariano's death — so close to salvation, yet too late.
