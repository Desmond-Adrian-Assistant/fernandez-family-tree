## CONTEXTUAL HISTORY RESEARCH

### Filipino Community in Chicago (1960s-1980s)

**Population Growth:**
- 1960: 3,554 Filipinos in Chicago (2,143 men, 1,411 women)
- 1970: 9,497 in Chicago; 12,654 in Illinois total
- 1980: 41,283 in Chicago area; 43,889 in Illinois
- 2000: 95,298 in Chicago metro area

**Key factors driving immigration:**
- **Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965** opened doors — occupational preferences enabled professionals (nurses, physicians) to qualify
- Over time, **family reunification** provisions became more significant
- By the 1970s, Filipinas outnumbered Filipinos (reversing pre-war gender imbalance)
- **57% of Filipino Illinoisans were college graduates** in the 1970s

**Occupations:**
- Pre-1965: Restaurants, clubs, homes, US Post Office clerks, Pullman Company service workers
- Post-1965: **Professionals dominated** — especially nurses and physicians
- Filipino physicians made up **24% of foreign physicians** entering the US in 1970
- Accountants like Bienvenido would have been part of the professional immigrant wave
- Filipino professionals often experienced **credential recognition challenges** — many had to re-certify

**Community Organizations:**
- **Filipino American Council of Chicago (FACC)** — umbrella organization since late 1940s
- **Dr. Jose Rizal Memorial Center** at 1332 West Irving Park Road — main gathering place since 1974
- Provincial/language-based associations (Ilocano, Pangasinan, etc.)
- **Anti-Martial Law movement** active in Chicago 1972-1986 (Marcos era)

**Relevance to Bienvenido:**
- Arrived ~1981, right in the peak growth period (population tripled from 1970-1980)
- As an accountant with a college degree, he fit the professional immigrant profile perfectly
- Family reunification likely played a role — someone in the family may have sponsored the petition
- Burial in **Niles, IL** — a northwest suburb popular with Filipino families

### Bienvenido's Career Path: Filipino Accountant in Chicago

- Philippine accountancy was modeled on the American system (US colonial legacy)
- Filipino CPAs had to pass US CPA exams or get credential recognition
- Many Filipino accountants in the 1970s-80s worked for:
  - Large accounting firms (Big Eight)
  - Healthcare institutions (hospitals with Filipino staff networks)
  - Small businesses in Filipino communities
  - Government agencies
- The Filipino professional network in Chicago was tight-knit — referrals and community connections were essential for job placement

### Clark Air Base Connection to Filipino Immigration

- **Clark Air Base** in Mabalacat, Pampanga — largest US military air base outside the US
- Located approximately **60 km south of Gerona, Tarlac** (where Bienvenido was born)
- Employed thousands of Filipino civilians as base workers
- Created a pipeline of Filipino workers familiar with American systems and culture
- Many Filipino base workers and their families used military connections to emigrate
- The base was a major economic engine for Central Luzon — Tarlac, Pampanga, and nearby provinces
- **Closed in 1991** after Mount Pinatubo eruption

**Potential connection:** Bienvenido, born in Gerona (60km from Clark), may have had family or community connections to Clark Air Base that facilitated eventual immigration to the US.

### Gerona, Tarlac: Post-War Reconstruction (1940s-1950s)

- **WWII devastation:** Japanese occupation followed by Allied liberation campaigns
- **USAFFE 21st Division** retreated through Gerona (documented in barangay histories)
- Post-independence (July 4, 1946): Focus on reconstruction
- Bienvenido was born in 1937 — he was 8 years old when the war ended
- He would have grown up during the reconstruction period
- Agricultural economy centered on rice farming
- Road infrastructure connecting to Pangasinan was rebuilt in the 1950s

### The Principalía Class: Economic Role

The Fernandez and Daquigan families occupied principalía (local elite) positions as tenientes del barrio:

- **Landholding:** Primary economic base was agricultural land — rice paddies, fishponds, and small-scale trade
- **Fishponds:** Common in Pangasinan (especially near Lingayen Gulf) — Pangasinan's name literally means "place of salt/fish"
- **Rice farming:** Tarlac and Pangasinan are major rice-producing provinces
- **Trade:** Principalía families often controlled local commerce, market stalls, and acted as middlemen between farmers and urban buyers
- **Education:** Principalía invested heavily in children's education — explains why Bienvenido became an accountant
- **Intermarriage:** Strategic marriages between principalía families preserved landholdings and political alliances
  - Mariano Fernandez (Danzo principalía) married Victoria Daquigan (Gerona political family)
  - Mariano's 2nd wife Dionicia Capinding came from the founding family of adjacent Tagumbao
